The Science of Spaced Repetition

What is Spaced Repetition?

Spaced repetition is a learning technique that leverages the psychological spacing effect‚Äîthe phenomenon where we learn more effectively when study sessions are spread out over time. This method is based on the work of German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus, who discovered that our memory of information follows a predictable forgetting curve.

By reviewing information at strategically timed intervals, we can interrupt the forgetting process and strengthen long-term memory retention. Each successful recall strengthens the neural pathways, making the information more durable and accessible over time.

üìà

The Forgetting Curve

Without review, we forget approximately 50% of new information within an hour, and up to 70% within 24 hours. Spaced repetition interrupts this curve by scheduling reviews just before the point of forgetting.

üéØ

Optimal Timing

Reviews are scheduled at increasing intervals: 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month. This pattern maximizes retention while minimizing study time.

‚ö°

Active Recall

Each review requires active effort to retrieve the information, strengthening memory pathways more effectively than passive re-reading.

üß†

Long-term Retention

This method can increase retention rates to over 95% for extended periods, making it ideal for building deep, lasting knowledge.

How Wisdom SRS Works

Our algorithm uses a modified SM-2 algorithm that adapts to your individual learning patterns. Each time you review a quote, you rate your difficulty level:

  • Again: You struggled with this quote - it will reappear soon
  • Hard: You found it challenging - review in a few days
  • Good: You remembered it well - review in a week
  • Easy: You knew it instantly - review in a month

The algorithm adjusts the scheduling based on your ratings, optimizing for your personal learning curve and ensuring you see each quote at the perfect moment for maximum retention.

Every quote in our collection has been carefully selected and contextualized to provide maximum learning value:

  • Historical Context: Each quote includes the philosopher's period and background
  • Modern Explanation: Clear interpretations that connect ancient wisdom to contemporary life
  • Difficulty Levels: Quotes are categorized by complexity and philosophical depth
  • Cross-References: Related concepts are linked to build comprehensive understanding

Philosophical Traditions

Greek Philosophy (6th-3rd century BCE)

The foundation of Western thought, featuring Socrates' method of questioning, Plato's theory of forms, and Aristotle's systematic approach to knowledge. These thinkers established the fundamental questions that philosophy still grapples with today.

Roman Stoicism (1st-2nd century CE)

Practical wisdom for daily life, emphasizing virtue, self-control, and acceptance of what we cannot change. Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, and Epictetus offer timeless guidance for resilience and inner peace.

Eastern Wisdom (6th-5th century BCE)

Ancient insights from Taoism and Confucianism, emphasizing harmony, balance, and the interconnectedness of all things. Lao Tzu and Confucius provide different but complementary approaches to living wisely.

Modern Philosophy (17th-20th century)

Contemporary perspectives on existence, consciousness, and meaning, building on ancient foundations while addressing modern challenges. From Descartes to Frankl, these thinkers bridge classical wisdom with modern understanding.
